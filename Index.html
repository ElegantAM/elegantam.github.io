Why this happens 🧠
Your brain constantly tries to subtract the lighting from what it sees to find the "true" color of an object. This is called color constancy.

The dress photo has ambiguous lighting — it could be in shadow (cool light) or in sunshine (warm light). Your brain makes a guess based on your personal visual history — then locks in. That guess becomes your reality.

Experiment 02
Two squares. Same colour. Your eyes refuse to believe it.
Square A looks dark. Square B looks light. They are the exact same shade of grey. This is the most famous optical illusion in neuroscience — created by MIT professor Edward Adelson.
Both squares are #787878
Identical RGB values. Your brain sees them as different because it computes brightness relative to neighbors — not absolutely. The shadow tells your brain "B must be lighter than it appears." So it brightens B in your perception.
This is not a flaw — it's a feature. In nature, this trick helps you identify objects correctly under different lighting. Your brain is so confident in this computation that even when you know they're the same, you still can't see it.

Against a dark background, orange looks lighter and brighter. Against a grey background, the same orange looks darker and more saturated.

Your brain doesn't measure color — it compares it. Always relative to surroundings. This is why interior designers say "never judge a paint color on a chip — always see it on the wall."
